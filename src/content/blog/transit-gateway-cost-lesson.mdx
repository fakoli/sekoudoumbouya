One of the most expensive assumptions in cloud infrastructure is that the architecture you inherited is the architecture you need. In my experience leading infrastructure at a large-scale consumer platform, I discovered that a core piece of the AWS networking setup -- Transit Gateway -- was generating significant costs in transit gateway and cross-VPC data transfer fees. By transitioning to direct VPC peering, the organization was able to eliminate that cost category entirely.

This is not a story about any specific company. It is a pattern I have seen repeated across organizations operating at scale, and the broader lesson applies regardless of where you work.

## The Starting Point

Transit Gateway is a common and well-understood pattern: it acts as a centralized hub connecting multiple VPCs, simplifying routing and providing a single point of control. AWS recommends it. Architecture diagrams feature it prominently. For many organizations, it is the right choice.

But defaults have costs, and at hyperscale traffic volumes, those costs can become significant.

## Understanding the Cost Model

For engineers who have not looked at this closely, here is the basic cost model. Transit Gateway charges you for two things: an hourly attachment fee for each VPC or VPN connection, and a per-GB data processing charge for every gigabyte that flows through the gateway.

That per-GB charge is the critical factor at scale. If you have hundreds of services communicating across VPCs, and all of that traffic routes through a Transit Gateway, you are paying a data processing fee on every byte. At high traffic volumes, those fees compound quickly.

Direct VPC peering, by contrast, does not have a data processing charge. You still pay for cross-AZ data transfer when applicable, but the Transit Gateway processing fee disappears entirely. The tradeoff is management complexity -- VPC peering connections are point-to-point, so you need more of them, and routing becomes more distributed.

## The Analysis Approach

The first step was not to propose a solution -- it was to understand the problem. That meant conducting a comprehensive analysis on AZ balances and data transfer patterns. Understanding where traffic actually flowed -- not where we assumed it flowed -- was essential.

The natural question was: what are we getting for the centralized model? The answer, when we broke it down, was centralized routing and simplified management. Those are real benefits. But they need to be weighed against the actual cost, and at hyperscale volumes, the math can shift dramatically.

## The Decision Process

Transitioning from a centralized to a distributed networking model has real implications:

**Routing complexity.** Transit Gateway gives you centralized route tables. With VPC peering, routing is managed per-connection. At scale, this means more configuration to manage. This can be mitigated through Infrastructure as Code -- mature Terraform CI/CD pipelines give you the ability to manage peering connections declaratively and consistently.

**Security boundaries.** Centralized connectivity can simplify security policies. Moving to a peering model means being more intentional about which VPCs can communicate with which. This often turns out to be a feature, not a bug -- it forces you to be more explicit about network boundaries.

**Operational risk.** Any networking change at scale carries risk. A phased migration -- validating each peering connection before decommissioning the corresponding Transit Gateway route -- is essential. This is not a "rip and replace."

**Team capability.** Can your engineering teams operate in a more distributed networking model? This is where prior investment in IaC standards and Terraform pipelines pays off. Good tooling abstracts enough complexity that teams do not need to become networking experts.

## The Broader Architecture Shift

The transition was part of a broader effort to build a networking foundation that combines centralized patterns where they make sense with distributed control mechanisms where they do not.

The key insight was that not every connection needs to go through a central gateway. Services that communicate frequently can be peered directly. Shared services that need broad reachability can retain centralized patterns where appropriate. Standardized centralized egress with distributed control patterns gives you the security benefits of controlled egress without the cost of routing all internal traffic through a central hub.

## Lessons for Other Teams

The specific numbers will vary by organization -- they are proportional to traffic volume. But the underlying lessons apply regardless of scale.

### Question your defaults regularly

Architectural decisions that were correct two years ago may not be correct today. Traffic patterns change. AWS pricing changes. Your team's capabilities change. Conduct periodic reviews of your core infrastructure assumptions, especially the ones that feel settled.

### Follow the money

Cost analysis is not just a FinOps exercise. When you see a large, growing cost line, it is telling you something about your architecture. The money points you to the problem.

### Investment in IaC pays compounding returns

You cannot make networking transitions safely without mature Terraform pipelines and IaC standards. The ability to declare, review, and test networking changes in code -- rather than clicking through the AWS console -- is what makes migrations of this scale feasible.

### Centralization is a spectrum, not a binary

The answer is rarely "centralized is bad, distributed is good." It is "centralize what benefits from centralization, distribute what benefits from distribution." Egress controls? Often centralized. Inter-service communication? Often better distributed via peering. The right answer depends on the specific use case.

## The Takeaway

The most expensive line item in your cloud bill might be an architectural decision that nobody has questioned in years. Questioning defaults -- especially the ones that feel settled -- is one of the highest-leverage activities an infrastructure engineer can do.

Every architecture decision has a shelf life. The question is whether you notice when it expires.
